THE GHOST OF ALICE WHITMORE

Psychic connection to the spirit realm. Murdered on October 7, 1925. She's been trying to communicate the truth for 100 years.
What she knows:
  1. She knows that Thaddeus Crane poisoned Sebastian's elixir with foxglove and that it killed Cordelia and Sebastian
  2. She knows someone murdered herâ€”she felt a shadowy figure approaching, then something heavy hit her head
  3. She knows about the affair between Thaddeus and herself
  4. She knows about Thomas (her brother) and Cordelia's relationship in 1924, and that Cordelia disappeared for months in early 1925

What she doesn't know: She doesn't know that Thaddeus Crane was the one who murdered her.

THE GHOST OF CORDELIA MONTROSE

Tragic bride of 1925. Died on October 18, 1925, never wearing her wedding dress. Engaged to Sebastian Crane, but something was wrong.
What she knows:
  1. She knows Thaddeus Crane was having an affair with Alice Whitmore and that she threatened him
  2. She was getting sicker for the past month
  3. News of Sebastian's and Alice's deaths recently arrived
  4. She knows about Eleanor (her daughter she had to give away) and Thomas (her first love, lost at sea)

What she doesn't know: She doesn't know that Thaddeus Crane was the one who murdered her, Sebastian, and Alice. She doesn't even know anything about the poison or the elixir.

THE GHOST OF SEBASTIAN CRANE (THE ALCHEMIST)

Alchemist and visionary. Died on October 11, 1925. He created an elixir formula, but it was corrupted. He unknowingly poisoned the woman he loved.
What he knows:
  1. He created the elixir formula and administered it to Cordelia starting September 4, 1925
  2. He knows Cordelia was getting sicker and he asked his brother Dr. Thaddeus for advice
  3. He tested the formula on himself and got violently ill
  4. He believes it was his own fault, his own incompetence that caused everything

What he doesn't know: He doesn't know that his brother Thaddeus corrupted the formula with foxglove. He doesn't know who really killed him or Cordelia.
